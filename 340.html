Description

Official course catalogue description: Advanced introduction to basic, non-measure theoretic probability covering topics in more depth and with more rigor than MATH 230. Topics include random variables with discrete and continuous distributions. Independence, joint distributions, conditional distributions, generating functions, Bayes’ formula, and Markov chains. Rigorous arguments are presented for the law of large numbers, central limit theorem, and Poisson limit theorems. Prerequisite: Mathematics 202, 212, or 222. Not open to those who have taken Mathematics 230 or Statistics 230.

My view: This course introduces probability as an area of mathematics. By that I mean probability is not developed solely as a tool or language for coomputations and how it can be applied to other disciplines but as a basic element in mathematics. A key component of this course is to frame probability and randomness in precise mathematical terms and understand what theorems and proofs for random objects mean.

As a statistician I will also highlight how probability is used to model random phenomena and make inferences from observations. Some of the fundamental mathematical results and tools we will consider include law of large numbers, central limit theorem, random walks and branching processes. We also develop an intution for the both the infinite and the infinitesimal in the context of random processes.

Contrast with 230: The goal of Math 230 is to develop compentence in probabilistic reasoning and using probability as a tool to solve problems and model random phenomena. Unlike 230, in this course abstractions and proofs will be central to the course. So it is a mater of taste and a matter of mathematical rigor in deciding between the courses.
